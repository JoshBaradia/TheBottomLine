## CHAPTER 4: PROJECT INTEGRATION MANAGEMENT

### What Is Project Integration Management?

Project integration management involves **coordinating all other project management knowledge areas** throughout a project's life cycle. It ensures all elements come together at the right time. It is the **only knowledge area that touches ALL FIVE phases** of project management (Initiating → Planning → Executing → Monitoring & Controlling → Closing).

The project manager's primary focus should be on integration management — seeing the big picture from 30,000 feet, not burrowing into technical details.

**Interface management** = identifying and managing the points of interaction between various project elements. Primary tools: **communication and relationships**.

---

### The 7 Processes of Project Integration Management

| # | Process | Phase | Key Output |
|---|---------|-------|------------|
| 1 | Develop the Project Charter | Initiating | Project Charter, Assumption Log |
| 2 | Develop the Project Management Plan (PMP) | Planning | PMP |
| 3 | Direct and Manage Project Work | Executing | Deliverables, WPD, Change Requests |
| 4 | Manage Project Knowledge | Executing | Lessons Learned Register |
| 5 | Monitor and Control Project Work | Monitoring & Controlling | WPR, Change Requests |
| 6 | Perform Integrated Change Control (PICC) | Monitoring & Controlling | Approved/Rejected/Held Changes |
| 7 | Close the Project or Phase | Closing | Final Report, Final Product Transition |

**Key tool & technique across almost all processes: Expert Judgment** — if you forget a T&T on the exam, expert judgment is almost always a safe answer (with a few exceptions the professor will show).

---

### Strategic Planning & Project Selection

**Strategic planning** = determining long-term objectives by analyzing strengths/weaknesses, studying opportunities/threats, predicting trends, and projecting needs.

**SWOT Analysis** — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Professor says: "Leave SWOT for undergrads. Use **PESTLE** analysis instead (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) — it's a more sophisticated, chronological way of documenting risk."

**Portfolio Management** = about the strategic direction and strategic imperative of the organization. What is the strategy to grow the organization?

**Four-stage IT project selection process:**
1. IT Strategy Planning → tie IT to mission/vision
2. Business Area Analysis → identify key business processes
3. Project Planning → define scope, benefits, constraints
4. Resource Allocation → select projects, assign resources

---

### Developing the Project Charter

The project charter is the document that **formally authorizes the project** and gives the project manager authority to obtain resources (physical and human) from functional managers.

**Key stakeholders should sign the project charter** to acknowledge agreement on the need and intent of the project.

**The project charter is a key output of the Initiating process.**

**Inputs for developing a project charter:**
- **Business Case** — the WHY. Why are we doing this project? (Comes BEFORE the project charter — don't confuse them!)
- **Agreements/Contracts** — once signed, binding by both parties
- **Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs)** — factors you have NO CONTROL over (government regulations, industry standards, regulatory environment, market conditions)
- **Organizational Process Assets (OPAs)** — processes, procedures, SOPs, templates, lessons learned, historical information that ARE available within the organization

**Tools & Techniques:** Expert judgment, data gathering, interpersonal and team skills, meetings

**Project Charter Contents:** Sponsor info, PM info, business case, stakeholder expectations, scope overview, approach (step-by-step in chronological order), objectives/goals.

**Waterfall vs. Agile Charter:**
- **Waterfall**: One-page (sometimes two) document with full details
- **Agile**: User story format — "As a [role], I want to [action], so that [benefit]"

---

### Project Success Criteria

A project is successful when it is: **On budget, on time, and meets stakeholder expectations.**

---

### Kickoff Meeting

Before starting a project, you need two things per PMI:
1. A **Project Charter**
2. A **Kickoff Meeting**

The kickoff meeting should include a representative from **every department** the project touches so no one can claim they were not part of the meeting. The sponsor may attend to convey urgency and relevance.

**Kickoff meeting tracking:** Date assigned, due date, attendance (yes/no), participation (yes/no), issues (current problems), risks (uncertainties), action items with responsible parties.

---

### Developing the Project Management Plan (PMP)

The PMP is a document used to **coordinate all project planning documents** and guide project execution and control.

**The PMP is PROGRESSIVELY ELABORATED** — you revisit, refine, and revise it as more information becomes available.

It contains subsidiary plans: communication management plan, risk management plan, schedule management plan, scope management plan, etc.

**Main function of creating a PMP: To guide project execution.**

**Those who will do the work should help plan the work** — the WBS should NOT be created by the PM alone. It is NOT a unilateral decision.

---

### Directing and Managing Project Work

- **Majority of time and money is spent in the Executing phase** — this is where resources (physical AND human) are consumed
- **Alpha project managers spend more time in Planning** → which means they spend LESS time and money in Executing
- The more time you spend planning, the less time you spend executing

**MBWA (Management By Walking Around / Gemba Walk):** An informal audit — you walk around, observe what the team is doing, get instantaneous feedback, and document it.

**Milestones:** A critical point in a project, usually with NO duration attached. After completing a phase, you celebrate. Example: finishing planning and moving to executing is a milestone.

**Deliverables:** Verifiable product, result, or capability to perform a service. What exactly are you giving at the end of a phase or project?

---

### Work Performance Data → Information → Reports (WPD → WPI → WPR)

This is CRITICAL — know the difference:

| Level | Name | What It Is | Example |
|-------|------|------------|---------|
| WPD | Work Performance **Data** | Raw data; tells you nothing by itself | Invoices coming in, # of hours logged, # of defects |
| WPI | Work Performance **Information** | Analysis of the data; converted into meaning | "We are 45% over budget" or "We are trending above last year" |
| WPR | Work Performance **Report** | **ACTIONABLE** — decisions are made here | "We need to cut 5 team members to stay on budget" |

**Which one is actionable? → WPR (Work Performance Report)**

---

### Managing Project Knowledge

Two types of knowledge:

| Type | Definition | How to Get It |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Explicit** | Easily explained using words, pictures, numbers; easy to communicate and store | Documents, project charter, reports |
| **Tacit** | Difficult to express; highly personal — beliefs, insight, experience | Person-to-person interaction; workshops; indirect observation over time |

**Knowledge Management** = connecting PEOPLE to PEOPLE
**Information Management** = connecting PEOPLE to INFORMATION

Similarity: both involve connecting. Difference: WHAT they connect.

Senior team members often guard tacit knowledge to protect their positions — you sometimes have to gather it indirectly over time.

---

### Monitoring and Controlling Project Work

**Variance (Delta)** = the difference between what was planned (in the PMP) and what is actually being done. Project managers do NOT like variance, delta, or risk.

**Baseline** = a starting point / measuring point. The current approved version of a document or plan. If an auditor finds you using an old revision, your system is not robust enough → NCR (Non-Conformity Report).

---

### Perform Integrated Change Control (PICC)

**Main objectives of PICC:**
1. Influencing factors that create changes to ensure changes are beneficial
2. Determining that a change has occurred
3. Managing actual changes as they occur
4. **Impact Assessment** — looking across the ENTIRE project to see how a change will affect everything else. Key question: "What impact will this have on the **triple constraint** (cost, schedule, scope)?"

**CCB (Change Control Board)** and **CCC (Change Control Committee):**
- Formal groups of people assigned to review ALL proposed changes
- They **approve, reject, or put on hold** any changes
- If you get a HOLD or REJECT: address their feedback, revise, and resubmit as a new proposed change (input back into the process)

**Primary function of the CCB:** To approve, reject, or hold changes on a project.

**Change Control System:** A formal, documented process describing when and how official project documents and work may be changed. It creates a paper trail.

**Configuration Management:** Ensures the descriptions of project products are correct and complete. Moving from Revision 9 → Revision 10 = configuration management. The **Configuration Management Specialist** is responsible for this.

**Exam scenario:** "You are working on Revision 12 and about to move to Revision 13. Who is responsible?" → Configuration Management Specialist.

---

### NCRs and CAPAs (Preview for Chapter 8 - Quality)

- **NCR (Non-Conformity Report):** When there is a deviation between what was supposed to be done and what was actually done — document it
- **CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action):** An escalation of NCRs. Used to get to the **root cause** of recurring issues

---

### Closing the Project or Phase

You MUST bring a project to closure even if it fails during executing. Why? So the information goes into the organization's **repository** for others to learn from — why the project was terminated, lessons learned, etc.

**Main input to closing:** Project Charter (provides high-level overview)
**Main T&T:** Expert judgment, data analysis, meetings (interview SMEs — fact-finding mission)

After closure: address the **warranty** period. Keep a small percentage of the team on staff to handle warranty issues.

---

### IT Project Phases (6 Phases — NOT in textbook but exam-relevant)

1. **Project Feasibility Study** — Go or No-Go decision
2. **Project Acquisition** — Purchase Order (PO) needed before work begins
3. **Project Initiation** — Create the project charter (can be considered pre-initiation)
4. **IT Project Planning** — Detailed planning
5. **Executing** — Planned activities performed; on budget, on time, meets requirements
6. **Project Closure** — Release resources, present to senior management, archive records in repository + WARRANTY

**Remember: 5 phases of PM, 10 knowledge areas, 6 phases of an IT project, triple constraint (3)**

## CHAPTER 5: PROJECT SCOPE MANAGEMENT

### What Is Scope?

**Scope** = ALL the work involved in creating the products of the project and the processes used to create them.

It tells you what the project IS about and what it is NOT about. Example: "This project is about distributing iPads to sales and marketing. It does NOT include anything about laptops."

---

### 6 Processes of Scope Management

| # | Process | Key Takeaway |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Plan Scope Management | How to create WBS, how to track, how to prioritize requirements |
| 2 | Collect Requirements | Interviews (fact-finding mission), focus groups, questionnaires, surveys, benchmarking |
| 3 | Define Scope | Description of what project IS and IS NOT; progressive elaboration |
| 4 | Create WBS | Tool = **Decomposition**; break down into smaller pieces |
| 5 | Validate Scope | **Formal acceptance** — signed off by sponsor/customer |
| 6 | Control Scope | Manage changes through the PICC process; NOT a unilateral decision |

---

### Collecting Requirements

Best methods: **interviews** with stakeholders (fact-finding mission), focus groups, facilitated workshops, questionnaires/surveys, observation, prototyping, benchmarking.

Professor's preference: **Interviewing** — you get firsthand information and stakeholders cannot later deny what they told you. Record the conversation if possible and send back for confirmation.

---

### Defining Scope

- Includes product scope description, acceptance criteria, and detailed deliverables
- Also document: boundaries, constraints, assumptions, supporting references
- **As time progresses, the scope should become clearer and more specific** = **Progressive Elaboration**

---

### Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

The WBS is a **deliverable-oriented grouping** of all work in a project that defines the **total scope**.

**Tool/Technique for creating WBS = DECOMPOSITION** — breaking down deliverables into smaller, more manageable pieces.

**Work Package** = the **lowest level** of the WBS. At this level, you should know:
- The number of hours it will take
- The pay scale / cost of the person doing it
- One person should be responsible for each work package

If one person can't handle a work package alone, break it down further.

**Critical WBS Rules:**

| Rule | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **100% Rule** | Everything beneath must add up to what's at the top. Nothing outside the WBS should be done |
| **Mutually Exclusive** | No duplicates anywhere in the WBS |
| **Cumulatively Exhaustive** | No gaps, no overlaps |
| **Not Unilateral** | Everyone working on the project should contribute to creating the WBS — NOT just the PM |

**WBS Dictionary:** A companion document to the WBS that provides additional detail (where work will be performed, when, by whom, etc.) because the WBS itself doesn't have enough space.

**Professor's requirement for your WBS:** Must include a **Legal section**, **Planning section**, and **Finance section** — you cannot run an IT or pharma project without legal involvement. (-3 points if missing)

---

### Gold Plating

**Gold Plating** = adding extra features or functionality that the customer did NOT ask for. This is a **warning/negative** — don't do it. If it's not in the scope and the sponsor didn't approve it, don't do it.

How to use it to your advantage: incorporate it into the scope before execution begins.

---

### Validating Scope

**The key takeaway: FORMAL ACCEPTANCE** — the sponsor or customer signs off on the deliverables. This must be documented (DocuSign, signature, etc.). Without formal acceptance, you cannot proceed.

---

### Controlling Scope

"The goal of scope control is to influence the factors that cause scope changes, to ensure that changes are processed according to procedures developed as part of integrated change control."

Any change to scope must go through the **PICC process** — it is NOT a unilateral decision.

**Scope Creep** = the tendency for project scope to keep getting bigger and bigger because stakeholders keep adding requirements informally (at water fountains, restaurants, hallways) without documenting changes. The scope balloons out of control.

**FDA 483 Letter:** If an organization gets a 483 from the FDA, it means they are not up to standard — they cannot export, release documents, etc. Relevant to ISO 27001 and quality audits.

## CHAPTER 6: PROJECT SCHEDULE MANAGEMENT

**Reading focus per professor: Pages 242–252, 260, and 264 (fast tracking & crashing).**

### Why Schedules Matter

Time management is often cited as the **main source of conflict** on projects. Time is the variable with the **least flexibility** — it passes regardless of what happens.

---

### 6 Processes of Schedule Management

| # | Process | Key Focus |
|---|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Plan Schedule Management | Policies, procedures, documentation for the schedule |
| 2 | Define Activities | Identify specific actions to produce deliverables (activity list + activity attributes) |
| 3 | Sequence Activities | Determine the order and dependencies |
| 4 | Estimate Activity Durations | How long each activity will take |
| 5 | Develop the Schedule | Analyze sequences, resources, durations → create the schedule |
| 6 | Control the Schedule | Manage changes; use burn down chart in Agile |

---

### Defining Activities

Identifying the specific actions that will produce project deliverables in enough detail to determine **resource and schedule estimates**.

**Activity List** = tabulation of activities (name, ID number, description) — think of it like a WBS
**Activity Attributes** = additional detail (predecessors, successors, relationships, leads/lags, resource requirements) — think of it like a WBS Dictionary

**Milestone** = a significant event that normally has **NO DURATION**. It marks progress. Examples: signing the project charter, completing a phase, customer sign-off.

---

### Sequencing Activities — Dependencies

Three types of dependencies:

| Type | Also Called | Meaning | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Mandatory** | Hard Logic | MUST be done in this order | Must have visa before entering Brazil; must show badge for access |
| **Discretionary** | Soft Logic | Either order works; defined by project team | Paint walls before or after laying carpet — your choice (but each has risk) |
| **External** | — | Depends on non-project activities | Foreign student needs visa from government before enrolling |

---

### Leads and Lags

| Concept | Definition | Key Point |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Lead** | The predecessor activity does NOT have to finish before the successor activity can begin | Overlapping tasks; resources ARE being used |
| **Lag** | There is a waiting period between predecessor and successor | Dead time; **NO resources are dispensed** during lag |

**Lag example:** Pouring cement — you must wait for it to dry before putting load-bearing items on it. During drying, no work can happen on that task. Cross-train team members so they can work on other tasks during lag time.

---

### Critical Path Method (CPM)

**Critical Path** = the **longest path** through a project network diagram, which determines the **shortest possible project duration**.

**Why it matters:** Any delay on the critical path **extends the entire project duration**. If your manager asks "Is it on the critical path?" — they're asking because if yes, the whole project timeline is at risk.

**How to find it:**
1. Identify ALL paths from start to finish
2. Add up the duration of each path
3. The path with the **highest total** = critical path

**Example from class:** Four paths — A,D,H,J / B,E,H,J / B,E,F,J / C,G,I,J — with durations 2+5+6+3 = 16 days (critical path).

**Exam question pattern:** "Your team has a problem with a task that will take 6 more weeks. Manager asks: Is it on the critical path?" → Because if yes, the project will be extended by 6 weeks.

---

### Estimating Activity Durations

- People doing the work SHOULD (MUST) help create estimates
- Always factor in skill level: a seasoned DBA might take 45 minutes; a new hire might take 1 hour 45 minutes
- **Always incorporate BUFFERS**

**Three-Point Estimate:**

| Estimate | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **Optimistic** | If everything goes perfectly |
| **Pessimistic** | If everything goes wrong |
| **Most Likely** | The realistic middle ground |

Never give just one number — always provide optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic.

**PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique):**
- **P = PROGRAM** (not Project!) — "Program Evaluation and Review Technique"
- Uses three-point estimates
- PERT weighted average = (Optimistic + 4×Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6

---

### Schedule Compression Techniques

When your project is behind schedule, two options:

| Technique | What You Do | Risk |
|-----------|------------|------|
| **Crashing** | Add MORE RESOURCES (more people, more laptops, etc.) | Higher cost; new people may not be trained; mistakes increase |
| **Fast Tracking** | Do activities SIMULTANEOUSLY (in parallel) | High risk; dependencies may cause problems |

Both add RISK to the project. The budget implications and quality risks must be weighed carefully.

---

### Buffers, Murphy's Law, and Parkinson's Law

**Buffer (Task)** = additional time to complete a TASK. Keep it confidential — don't share with everyone or they'll use up the extra time.

**Project Buffer** = additional time added before the PROJECT'S DUE DATE. Different from a task buffer.

**Don't confuse them on the exam:** Buffer → task level. Project buffer → overall project deadline.

**Murphy's Law** = "If something can go wrong, it WILL go wrong." Always plan for it. Always build buffers.

**Parkinson's Law** = "Work expands to fill the time allotted." If you give someone 2 hours for a 1-hour task, they WILL take the full 2 hours. This is the employee you do NOT want to hire. The ideal employee finishes in 1 hour and asks "What else can I do?"

**Exam tip from professor:** "When you sit for the exam, remember Parkinson's Law. You do NOT want to hire him."

---

### Legalization of Documents (Not in textbook — real world)

When working on international IT projects, you need:
- **Certificate of Agency and Distribution** (exclusive agent agreement)
- Must be attested by **Chamber of Commerce** and **Country Embassy**
- Document legalization timelines: Notarization (1 day), Apostille (10 days), US State Dept seal (6–9 weeks), Country embassy legalization (2–3 days)

**Why this matters for scheduling:** These timelines MUST be factored into your buffers. If apostille takes 10 days, your buffer must extend past that.
